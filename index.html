SECTION 1 / 7The partners' track record

This is the core of the whole exercise. NovaX has no history — the partners do. Every credibility claim in the profile traces back to what you write here. Be precise. Only include what you would defend across the table from a procurement committee.

1.The partners — name, role at NovaX, and career before NovaX.
For each: previous employers, job titles, and years. Exactly as it should appear in a prequalification file.

Partner 2

2.The specific projects. Which projects did the partners work on, in what role, at what scale?
Scale means numbers a buyer checks: contract value, built-up area, workforce managed, number of assets. If a number is an estimate, write "approx." next to it.
3.Why does NovaX exist? The partners left senior positions to start this — what did they see?
This becomes the company story. Answer like you're explaining it to a client over coffee, not writing a brochure.
4.Can you provide CVs, completion certificates, or reference letters that back up the track record?
Classification and prequalification files ask for evidence, not statements. What exists on paper?
SECTION 2 / 7Services

The profile must separate what NovaX delivers today from what it plans to deliver. Mixing the two is how new companies get disqualified.

5.What can NovaX deliver today, with current staff and equipment, if a contract started Monday?
Tick only what's real. Anything you'd deliver through a subcontractor still counts — the delivery model comes next.
Hard FM — technical
Soft FM — support
Management & integration
6.Delivery model in year one — own workforce or subcontractors?
Buyers ask this directly. The answer shapes how services are described.
7.If NovaX signed a contract tomorrow needing 100+ staff on site, how fast could you mobilize?
Buyers price mobilization risk on new companies heavily. Be realistic — visas, recruitment, equipment.
8.What systems does NovaX actually have today — not planned, have?
If "technology" becomes part of the pitch, this is what stands behind it. A named system is a claim; an intention is not.
9.What is planned for the next 12–24 months but NOT real yet?
This goes on the "cannot say yet" side of the claims inventory so nobody accidentally writes it as fact.

SECTION 4 / 7Real differentiators

Every FM company says "quality" and "commitment". These questions dig for what NovaX can actually promise and keep.

13.What do clients complain about with current FM providers? Tick what you've seen first-hand.
14.Of the problems you just ticked — which will NovaX concretely fix, and how?
Specifics only. "Better service" is not an answer. A number, a named commitment, a structural choice is.
15.Pick a maximum of THREE you can defend in a bid clarification meeting today.
If you tick more than three, the profile says nothing. These become the three pillars of the messaging.
SECTION 5 / 7The competition

Positioning is relative. Knowing who you lose to, and why, tells us where NovaX can honestly win.

16.In year one, who do you expect to lose bids to?
17.Which company in the market do you actually respect — the one NovaX should operate like in 5 years?
18.Who overpromises? What do competitors say in bids or brochures that you refuse to copy?
This directly shapes the tone of voice — what NovaX will never sound like.
SECTION 6 / 7Ambition

The profile must aim where NovaX is going without claiming it's already there.

19.What contract sizes should NovaX compete for in the first three years?
20.Three years from now — what should NovaX be known as?
21.Can NovaX issue bid bonds and performance bonds — and up to what contract size?
Government buyers shortlist on financial capacity. A company that can't bond can't sign. This sets the ceiling on Q19 — if the answers conflict, the profile targets the bondable number.
